# Staff system grouping in Optical Music Recognition (OMR)

Specific problem: grouping staves into *systems* (the grand staff for piano) and page-level layout analysis.

This is a well-studied problem that sits at the "layout analysis" stage of an Optical Music Recognition (OMR) pipeline. For solo piano specifically, a "system" is a grand staff — two staves (treble + bass) joined by a brace and played in sync — so the task decomposes into detecting staff lines, grouping them into staves, and then grouping staves into systems. Here's what the literature offers, from classic geometric methods through modern deep learning.

## The classic geometric pipeline

Nearly every pre-2018 system follows the four-stage framework codified by Rebelo et al. (preprocessing → symbol recognition → notation reconstruction → final representation), and staff/system detection lives in the first stage. Algorithms for detection and removal of staff lines are a core part of that preprocessing stage. The geometric approach generally runs in three sub-steps:

**1. Scale estimation via run-length encoding.** Before anything else, you estimate two reference quantities: staff-line thickness and the interline distance (staff-space height). The standard trick is to encode each column of the binary image with run-length encoding — the most common black run approximates staff-line thickness and the most common white run estimates staff-space height. Audiveris does exactly this with two histograms: a "combo" histogram of each black run plus its following white run yields the interline, and a black-run histogram yields line thickness (with a second peak revealing beam thickness). These values then scale every downstream threshold.

**2. Staff-line detection.** The simplest classical method is the horizontal projection profile: accumulate black pixels per row into a histogram, and if lines are straight and horizontal, a staff appears as five consecutive distinct peaks. In practice the projection is computed at several small rotation angles to cope with staves that aren't perfectly horizontal. This is what the MIT cadenCV project and many teaching implementations use. The weakness is well known — scanned-from-book pages have staff lines that curve near the spine, and because staff lines are thin, any distortion or noise badly disrupts them, so plain line detectors like the Hough transform are insufficient.

**3. Grouping staves into systems.** Once individual staves (groups of five lines) are found, they must be merged into systems. The classical cue is barlines: a vertical segment that spans the gap between two staves indicates they belong to the same system. Audiveris is the clearest documented example of this logic in a production engine.

## How a production engine does system grouping (Audiveris)

Audiveris is worth studying because its "GRID" step does precisely what you're asking about. Its GRID step retrieves staff lines, barlines, systems, and parts in one stage. Internally it represents line candidates as filaments — long glyphs modeled as natural splines (sequences of Bézier curves), used for both horizontal staff-line candidates and vertical barline candidates, connected into a grid of possibly wavy lines that becomes the coordinate reference, absorbing skew and warp without separate preprocessing.

For the actual grouping: detected staves are gathered into systems based on the barlines found on the left side of the staves. Then, to recognize that two staves form a single piano part (a grand staff), it uses two combined cues — staves joined by a brace are also joined by "connectors" (vertical concrete segments joining two barlines) further along the staves; when both conditions hold, the two (rarely three) staves are treated as the same instrument such as piano or organ, i.e. a single part. This brace-plus-connector logic is robust enough that the main documented failure mode is a brace cropped off by a bad scan, which is handled by manual correction. Conceptually: a part is one, two (piano), or three (organ) staves, and a system is a vertical collection of parts played in sync, with systems played one after another down the page.

For solo piano this is the easy case in principle — every system is a two-staff grand staff, so once you've found staves you pair them top-to-bottom and validate each pair with the brace/barline connector. The system's bounding box then spans vertically from the top line of the upper staff to the bottom line of the lower staff (with margin added for ledger lines and tall symbols) and horizontally across the engraved width between the left brace/barline and the right system barline. The brace/bracket convention is standard: braces connect the right-hand and left-hand staves of piano/keyboard music into the combined two-staff unit called a grand staff.

## Robust staff detection for degraded images: stable paths

Because projection profiles fail on curved, broken, or skewed lines, the most influential classical robustness improvement is the **stable-paths / connected-path** method of Cardoso, Capela, Rebelo and Guedes (ICIP 2008; IEEE TPAMI 2009). The idea: treat the whole score as a graph, define the left and right page margins as two regions, and find shortest (lowest-cost) paths connecting them — staff lines emerge as connected paths from left to right across the page. It's a general-purpose, knowledge-free method, and "stable paths" are paths with a high probability of belonging to a staff line. Its appeal is intrinsic robustness — it naturally handles skew, discontinuities, and curvature without the chain of heuristic corrections earlier methods needed, completing missing pieces automatically and tolerating lines as thin as one pixel, with a post-processing step to remove accidental line intersections. It was validated on 2,688 synthetic images plus 40 real scores and consistently outperformed established algorithms. A later extension introduced "strong staff pixels" to generalize the cost function to grayscale images directly, avoiding binarization error. This remains a strong baseline if you want a non-learning approach.

## Deep learning: detecting systems as object-detection / segmentation targets

Since ~2018 the field has largely reframed staff and system finding as object detection or pixel segmentation, which often makes explicit staff *removal* unnecessary.

**Direct object detection of structural elements.** The paper most precisely on your target is Kletz & Pacha, "Detecting Staves and Measures in Music Scores with Deep Learning" (WoRMS 2021). They train a neural network to detect three structural classes at once — staves, measures, and "system measures" — reporting mAP over 75% detecting all three jointly and over 90% detecting each individually, on handwritten (MUSCIMA++) and typeset (AudioLabs v2) corpora. The "system measure" class is essentially the bounding box covering all staves of a system for one measure; the union of a row of system measures gives the system bounding box. They also describe a useful bootstrapping workflow — training on MUSCIMA++ first, then auto-annotating 940 AudioLabs images and manually correcting them to publish AudioLabs v2. The motivation, from the related measure-detector work, is that a universal staff detector combined with a robust measure detector is itself a useful framework for navigating scores, and going from measures to staves is a small step.

The generic detectors used across OMR — Faster R-CNN, RetinaNet, R-FCN, SSD, YOLO, and the Deep Watershed Detector — were benchmarked by Pacha et al. and Tuggener et al. Faster R-CNN/R-FCN/SSD reached ~80% mAP on music symbols, but full-page processing was an open issue handled with sliding windows. A caveat specific to music: orthogonal bounding boxes are a poor fit for objects that are long, thin, and possibly rotated, which is why DeepScoresV2 introduced oriented bounding boxes. For staves and systems this matters less (they're large and roughly axis-aligned) than for slurs and beams, which is part of why staves/systems are among the *easier* things to detect reliably.

**Segmentation approaches.** Rather than boxes, many systems classify each pixel. Calvo-Zaragoza et al. used a CNN to classify each pixel as symbol, staff, or background directly on grayscale images, outperforming state-of-the-art staff-removal algorithms on CVC-MUSCIMA. For layout analysis specifically, the dominant approach uses Selectional Auto-Encoders (a U-Net-like fully convolutional net): SAEs extract single-staff regions, which are then fed to a CRNN for staff-level recognition. A 2025 refinement adds uncertainty estimation — Monte Carlo Dropout aggregates multiple stochastic predictions to retrieve staff-region bounding boxes, cutting relative error 63.6% over the baseline and improving most at staff edges where conventional models err. Instance segmentation with Mask R-CNN has also been applied, with a parallel morphological staff-detection step added to infer pitch.

**A piano-aware hybrid worth noting.** TrOMR's Staff Detection Module is a clean template for exactly your use case: it runs semantic segmentation to produce a staff mask (staves and symbols as foreground), and crucially computes a bounding box that covers all symbols associated with the staff. Simultaneously it predicts a full-page layout label by examining the accolade/brace on the left side, classifying into one-staff system, two-staves system, or other. The two-staff label is the grand-staff/piano case directly, and detecting it from the left-margin brace mirrors the classical Audiveris cue but learned.

## The end-to-end piano line of work (context, not boxes)

Recent pianoform research has moved toward skipping explicit boxes, which is useful context for deciding how much box-precision you actually need. The GrandStaff dataset provides ~53,882 single-system piano scores, and FP-GrandStaff provides 688 full-page piano images from six composers drawn from the Humdrum repository. The Sheet Music Transformer / SMT++ transcribes full pages autoregressively. Notably, even these "segmentation-free" models acknowledge the system-reading-order problem: polyphonic systems are groups of staves that must be read simultaneously, so attention must jump top-to-bottom across the staves of a system, and they bootstrap from single-system training before curriculum-learning up to full pages. So the system unit remains central even when boxes are implicit.

## Datasets and evaluation for your task

The directly relevant annotated resources: AudioLabs v2 and MUSCIMA++ (the latter handwritten) carry stave/measure/system-measure boxes; DeepScoresV2 and DoReMi provide typeset pages with bounding boxes and pixel masks for hundreds of thousands of objects; and the 2025 Sheet Music Benchmark is the most modern option — 685 pages where regions for individual staves or musical systems are delimited with bounding boxes and labeled, supporting both staff-level and page-level layout analysis. Standard metric is mean Average Precision (mAP), though note the field has flagged that mAP and orthogonal boxes are imperfect for music's mix of very large and very small, scale-variant symbols — again, less of a problem for system-level boxes than for symbol-level.

## Practical recommendation for binarized solo-piano pages

Given that your input is already black-and-white bitmaps of *printed* (not handwritten) *solo piano* music, the problem is on the easier end:

A robust classical pipeline is very achievable and explainable: RLE scale estimation → staff-line detection (stable paths if any curvature/skew, else projection profiles) → cluster the lines into staves of five → pair consecutive staves into grand-staff systems, validating each pair with a left-margin brace and/or a barline connector spanning the inter-staff gap → emit the system box with a margin for ledger lines. This is essentially the Audiveris GRID logic, which is open source (AGPL) and directly inspectable.

If you prefer learning-based and want minimal engineering, fine-tuning an object detector to predict a single "system" (or "two-staff system") class is well-supported by the Kletz–Pacha results and the SMB/AudioLabs annotations, and the printed-piano regularity (every system is two staves) makes the class highly consistent. The TrOMR staff-detection module is the closest published design to "predict a box around the whole grand staff plus a two-staff layout label."
